The Problem Stack

Eight layers.
One compounding crisis.

Every layer of this problem feeds the next. Most organizations treat the symptoms — hotel bills, staffing gaps, high turnover — without ever reaching the root. Here's the full stack.

01
Root Problem
Labour Mobility
Healthcare systems and construction projects require specialized workers to relocate for weeks or months at a time. Hospitals in Prince George need nurses from Vancouver. Pipeline work in Fort McMurray pulls crews from BC. Highway projects need engineers two provinces from home.

This is not a housing problem yet. It's a logistics problem — with housing as the constraint.
02
Market Failure
The Housing Market Doesn't Serve This Need
Hotels are $180–$220/night and built for two-night stays. Long-term leases require 12-month commitments. Airbnb is inconsistent with no invoice structure. Company-owned housing is CapEx-heavy and operationally complex.

There is no purpose-built, institutionally structured, flexible housing product for workforce deployment at scale. That product simply doesn't exist.
03
Budget Impact
The Cost Is Crushing Project Economics
At ~$200/night, 10 staff for 90 days costs $180,000 in accommodation alone. That number scales linearly. Healthcare authorities face permanent budget pressure. Construction firms eat the margin or pad the bid. Either way, someone loses — and it compounds year over year.
$200/night × 10 staff × 90 days = $180,000. Before a single tool is picked up.
04
Operational Drag
The Admin Burden Is Invisible But Real
HR departments weren't built to run accommodation logistics. Project managers are booking hotels between site visits. Finance teams receive dozens of individual receipts per deployment. No standardization. No visibility. No leverage.

The hidden cost of workforce housing isn't just the room rate — it's the organizational drag it creates every single day.
05
Human Cost
Isolation Destroys the Individual
Workers in transient hotels aren't just uncomfortable — they're cut off. From routine, from community, from the conditions that make human beings function well. No kitchen means poor nutrition and higher sick days. No shared space means no decompression. No community means anxiety, loneliness, and early burnout.

Social isolation accelerates burnout. And burnout in healthcare and skilled trades doesn't just cost the employer — it costs the patient on the table and the structure being built.
This is the human cost that never shows up on a hotel invoice.
06
Team Cost
Burnout Destroys the Team
One burnt-out worker doesn't just leave — they lower the ceiling for everyone around them. Exhausted workers make more errors. Low morale spreads laterally. Without peer support, high-performers quietly disengage. Teams that live together badly, work together badly.

When the team has nowhere good to land at the end of the day, the work suffers.
07
Talent Strategy
Poor Housing Kills Attraction and Retention
Word travels. Workers talk. "They put us up in a Holiday Inn for four months" is not a recruiting pitch. Skilled workers have options — they take assignments where the conditions are better. Re-deployment rates collapse when the first rotation was miserable. Referrals dry up. And healthcare and trades are small worlds: reputations for poor worker care follow organizations for years.
Every organization fighting a staffing shortage is also fighting the reputation its accommodation decisions built.
08
Market Gap
The Procurement Problem
Even organizations that want to do this better can't — because there's no vendor built for it. No company that holds a national contract, moves fast, and invoices like a supplier. No platform that handles the quote → booking → contract → invoice pipeline cleanly. No single point of accountability when something goes wrong at 2am.

The market failure is complete: the problem is understood, the need is urgent, and the solution doesn't exist at scale.
We didn't build a housing company.
We built the solution to all eight layers.
